# 1. General Clinical Principles and Patient Safety

## 1.1. Patient-centered care
- Always verify patient identity before sharing or recording sensitive data.
- Use plain language; confirm understanding via teach-back.
- Document allergies, current medications, and major comorbidities.
- When in doubt about an urgent issue, advise the patient to seek immediate in-person evaluation or call emergency services.

## 1.2. Evidence & sources
- Base recommendations on up-to-date authoritative guidelines (WHO, CDC, national societies). This file is a compiled reference; before clinical use, cross-check specific dosing/indications with current guidelines.

## 1.3. Medication safety
- Check allergies and drug–drug interactions before recommending medications.
- Use weight-based dosing for many pediatrics and some adult medications (e.g., enoxaparin). If weight is unknown, do not estimate—ask the patient.
- Avoid giving controlled-substance prescriptions without a verified in-person evaluation, local regulatory compliance, and proper documentation.

# 2. Diagnostic Approach & Triage

## 2.1. History (SOAP-style brief guide)
- **S**ubjective: Chief complaint, HPI (onset, location, duration, character, aggravating/relieving factors, timing, severity), past medical history, medications, allergies, social history (tobacco/alcohol/drugs), family history.
- **O**bjective: Vital signs, focused physical exam findings, point-of-care tests if available.
- **A**ssessment: Differential diagnoses (list from most likely to less likely).
- **P**lan: Tests, medications, red flags and follow-up plan.

## 2.2. Triage levels (brief)
- **Emergency**: Airway compromise, severe respiratory distress, hemodynamic instability, unresponsive, stroke signs, ongoing chest pain with ischemia features, severe trauma.
- **Urgent (see same day)**: High fever in vulnerable patients, suspected appendicitis, severe but stable pain, signs of infection in immunocompromised.
- **Routine**: Mild symptoms, stable vital signs, medication refills without alarming features.

# 3. Vital Signs and Interpretation

- **Temperature:** Normal 36.1–37.2 °C (97–99 °F). Fever >38 °C (100.4 °F). Hypothermia <35 °C.
- **Pulse:** Adult resting 60–100 bpm. Tachycardia >100 bpm. Bradycardia <60 bpm (context dependent).
- **Blood pressure:** Normal ~120/80 mmHg. Hypertension generally ≥140/90 mmHg (depends on guideline and age). Hypotension systolic <90 mmHg is concerning.
- **Respiratory rate:** Normal 12–20 breaths/min. Tachypnea >20.
- **SpO2:** Normal ≥95% on room air. Hypoxemia <92% requires assessment; <90% often needs urgent supplemental oxygen.

# 4. Common Diseases

> Each disease entry includes: definition/overview, common causes/risk factors, presenting symptoms, key signs/tests, diagnostic approach, management (initial outpatient), red flags, prevention.

---

## 4.1 Infectious Diseases

### Upper Respiratory Tract Infection (Common cold)
- **Overview:** Viral infections of the nasal passages and pharynx (rhinoviruses, coronaviruses, seasonal viruses).
- **Symptoms:** Nasal congestion, rhinorrhea, sore throat, sneezing, mild cough, low-grade fever possible.
- **Diagnosis:** Clinical.
- **Management:** Supportive care: rest, hydration, antipyretics (paracetamol/acetaminophen, ibuprofen if no contraindication), saline nasal irrigation. Avoid antibiotics unless bacterial complication suspected (e.g., purulent sinusitis >10 days or severe symptoms).
- **Red flags:** High fever >39°C, severe shortness of breath, inability to hydrate, symptoms lasting >14 days without improvement.

### Influenza (seasonal)
- **Overview:** Respiratory illness caused by influenza A/B viruses.
- **Symptoms:** Sudden fever, myalgia, headache, cough, sore throat, fatigue.
- **Diagnosis:** Rapid influenza diagnostic tests (RIDT) or clinical in high-prevalence settings.
- **Management:** Antiviral therapy (e.g., oseltamivir) when indicated for high-risk individuals or severe disease and ideally started within 48 hours of symptom onset. Supportive care otherwise.
- **Prevention:** Annual influenza vaccine.
- **Red flags:** Severe respiratory distress, dehydration, altered mental status.

### COVID-19 (SARS-CoV-2)
- **Overview:** Viral respiratory disease with a spectrum from asymptomatic to severe pneumonia and multisystem involvement.
- **Symptoms:** Fever, cough, dyspnea, anosmia/ageusia, fatigue, GI symptoms.
- **Diagnosis:** PCR or rapid antigen testing.
- **Management:** Follow up-to-date public health guidance for isolation, testing, and antiviral or monoclonal antibody eligibility; supportive care for mild disease. For severe disease, hospitalize and consider oxygen, steroids for hypoxemic patients, antivirals as per current guidance.
- **Red flags:** Persistent hypoxemia, chest pain, severe dyspnea, confusion.

### Tuberculosis (TB) — pulmonary
- **Overview:** Mycobacterium tuberculosis infection, often chronic cough, weight loss, night sweats.
- **Diagnosis:** Sputum smear/culture, nucleic acid amplification tests (NAAT), chest imaging.
- **Management:** Multidrug anti-TB therapy per national program; public health notification and contact tracing.
- **Red flags:** Hemoptysis, progressive weight loss, severe respiratory compromise.

### Urinary Tract Infection (Uncomplicated cystitis)
- **Overview:** Bacterial infection of the bladder, commonly E. coli.
- **Symptoms:** Dysuria, frequency, urgency, suprapubic discomfort; usually no fever.
- **Diagnosis:** Urinalysis (nitrites, leukocyte esterase) and urine culture if complicated or recurrent.
- **Management:** Short-course oral antibiotics guided by local antibiogram (commonly nitrofurantoin, trimethoprim-sulfamethoxazole where susceptible, fosfomycin single dose). Encourage hydration and analgesia.
- **Red flags:** Fever, flank pain, sepsis, pregnancy, male sex, urinary retention — consider imaging and specialist care.

### Skin & Soft Tissue Infection — Cellulitis
- **Overview:** Bacterial infection of skin/subcutaneous tissue (commonly streptococci, S. aureus).
- **Symptoms:** Localized erythema, warmth, swelling, pain; may have fever.
- **Diagnosis:** Clinical; ultrasound if abscess suspected.
- **Management:** Oral antibiotics covering streptococci and MSSA for mild-to-moderate disease; IV antibiotics and hospitalization if systemic signs. Incision and drainage if abscess.
- **Red flags:** Rapid spread, systemic toxicity, limb-threatening infection, necrosis.

## 4.2 Cardiovascular

### Hypertension (Essential)
- **Overview:** Persistent elevation of blood pressure; risk factor for stroke, MI, heart failure.
- **Diagnosis:** Multiple readings on separate occasions; consider ambulatory/home BP monitoring to confirm.
- **Initial management:** Lifestyle changes (salt reduction, weight loss, exercise, moderation of alcohol), start antihypertensive medication when indicated per risk and BP thresholds. First-line agents: ACE inhibitors, ARBs, calcium-channel blockers, thiazide diuretics (choice guided by comorbidities).
- **Red flags:** Hypertensive emergency (severe BP elevation with target-organ damage) — requires immediate hospitalization.

### Ischemic Heart Disease (Stable angina)
- **Overview:** Chest discomfort due to myocardial ischemia.
- **Diagnosis:** History, ECG (resting or exercise stress testing), imaging.
- **Management:** Antianginal therapy (sublingual nitroglycerin for acute relief), beta-blockers, statins, antiplatelet therapy (aspirin) as indicated, risk modification, and cardiology referral for diagnostics/interventions.
- **Red flags:** Ongoing chest pain at rest, syncope, signs of heart failure.

### Heart Failure — chronic
- **Overview:** Syndrome of reduced cardiac output and/or elevated intracardiac pressures causing dyspnea, fatigue, edema.
- **Management:** Diuretics for fluid control, ACEi/ARB/ARNI, beta-blocker, mineralocorticoid receptor antagonist where indicated; follow guideline-directed medical therapy and monitor electrolytes/renal function.
- **Red flags:** Severe dyspnea at rest, hypotension, rising creatinine with diuretic therapy.

## 4.3 Endocrine / Metabolic

### Diabetes Mellitus Type 2
- **Overview:** Chronic hyperglycemia due to insulin resistance and relative insulin deficiency.
- **Diagnosis:** HbA1c ≥6.5%, fasting plasma glucose ≥126 mg/dL, or 2-h plasma glucose ≥200 mg/dL on OGTT.
- **Initial management:** Lifestyle modification (diet, exercise, weight loss), metformin as first-line unless contraindicated. Individualize glycemic targets; consider SGLT2 inhibitors or GLP-1 receptor agonists in patients with cardiovascular or renal disease.
- **Acute complication — hypoglycemia:** Treat with oral glucose if conscious; IV dextrose or intramuscular glucagon if unconscious.
- **Red flags:** Hyperosmolar hyperglycemic state or diabetic ketoacidosis — urgent hospital treatment.

## 4.4 Respiratory

### Asthma (adult)
- **Overview:** Reversible airway obstruction with variable symptoms: wheeze, cough, breathlessness.
- **Diagnosis:** Clinical plus spirometry showing reversible airflow limitation (≥12% and 200 mL improvement in FEV1 after bronchodilator).
- **Management:** Inhaled corticosteroids as baseline anti-inflammatory therapy; short-acting beta-agonists for relief; add-on controllers per severity.
- **Exacerbation:** Assess severity: oxygen for hypoxia, nebulized bronchodilators, systemic steroids for moderate/severe exacerbations. Follow a written action plan.
- **Red flags:** Silent chest, inability to speak full sentences, exhaustion, rising PaCO2 — emergency.

### COPD
- **Overview:** Persistent airflow limitation, commonly linked to smoking.
- **Diagnosis:** Spirometry with post-bronchodilator FEV1/FVC <0.70.
- **Management:** Smoking cessation, bronchodilators (short- and long-acting), pulmonary rehab, vaccinations, oxygen therapy in chronic hypoxemia.
- **Red flags:** Severe exacerbation with hypoxemia or respiratory failure.

---

## 4.5 Gastrointestinal

### Gastroesophageal Reflux Disease (GERD)
- **Overview:** Reflux of gastric contents causing heartburn, regurgitation.
- **Management:** Lifestyle measures (elevate head, avoid late meals, weight loss), short-term antacids/alginate, H2 blockers or proton-pump inhibitors (PPIs) for persistent disease. Refer for alarm features (weight loss, dysphagia, GI bleeding).

### Acute Appendicitis (brief)
- **Presentation:** Periumbilical pain migrating to right lower quadrant, anorexia, nausea/vomiting, fever.
- **Diagnosis:** Clinical exam, CBC, imaging (ultrasound or CT depending on availability/age/pregnancy).
- **Management:** Surgical consult for appendectomy; IV fluids, antibiotics as pre-op per local protocol.

---

## 4.6 Neurology

### Stroke (acute ischemic)
- **Overview:** Sudden focal neurological deficit due to vascular cause.
- **Initial approach:** FAST mnemonic (Facial droop, Arm weakness, Speech difficulty, Time to call emergency services). Immediate emergency evaluation; if within therapeutic window, consider thrombolysis and advanced stroke care pathways.
- **Red flags:** Any acute focal neurological deficit.

### Seizures & Epilepsy
- **Overview:** Unprovoked seizures may indicate epilepsy; first-time seizure requires careful workup.
- **Management:** For status epilepticus, follow emergency protocol (benzodiazepines first-line, then second-line agents). For recurrent seizures, start maintenance therapy after specialist assessment.

---

## 4.7 Musculoskeletal

### Osteoarthritis (OA)
- **Overview:** Degenerative joint disease causing joint pain, stiffness, reduced function.
- **Management:** Exercise and weight management, topical/oral analgesics (paracetamol, NSAIDs), joint injections or surgery for advanced disease.

### Rheumatoid Arthritis (RA)
- **Overview:** Autoimmune inflammatory polyarthritis requiring early disease-modifying therapy (DMARDs).
- **Management:** Refer to rheumatology for initiation of DMARDs (e.g., methotrexate) and immunomodulators.

---

## 4.8 Dermatology

### Eczema (Atopic Dermatitis)
- **Overview:** Chronic pruritic inflammatory skin condition.
- **Management:** Emollients, topical corticosteroids for flares, topical calcineurin inhibitors for sensitive areas; identify and avoid triggers.

### Acne Vulgaris
- **Overview:** Follicular hyperkeratinization, sebum, Propionibacterium acnes involvement.
- **Management:** Topical retinoids, benzoyl peroxide, topical/oral antibiotics for inflammatory disease, oral isotretinoin for severe nodulocystic acne with specialist monitoring.

---

## 4.9 Mental Health

### Depression (major depressive disorder)
- **Overview:** Persistent low mood, anhedonia, sleep/appetite changes, concentration issues.
- **Screening:** PHQ-9 questionnaire.
- **Management:** Psychotherapy (CBT) and/or medication (SSRIs) depending on severity; safety assessment for suicidal ideation is essential—immediate escalation if present.

### Anxiety Disorders
- **Overview:** Excessive worry or fear causing functional impairment.
- **Management:** CBT, SSRIs or SNRIs, short-term benzodiazepines only in selected cases and for short duration; avoid long-term benzodiazepines when possible.

---

## 4.10 Pediatrics (selected common topics)

### Febrile illness in children
- **Initial approach:** Check hydration, behavior, fever pattern. Antipyretics for comfort. Identify red flags: difficulty breathing, poor feeding, lethargy, convulsions.

### Growth & Development screening
- Use milestone checklists; refer early for suspected developmental delay.

---

## 4.11 Obstetrics & Gynecology (selected)

### Early pregnancy care
- Confirm pregnancy, estimate gestational age, baseline blood tests (blood group, Hb, infectious disease screening), folic acid supplementation recommended preconception and in early pregnancy.

### Warning signs in pregnancy
- Vaginal bleeding, reduced fetal movements after 28 weeks, severe abdominal pain, severe hypertension symptoms — urgent evaluation.

# 5. Medications — Common Classes & Notable Examples

> **Important:** Medication lists below include general adult dosing ranges intended as **reference only**. Cross-check doses for age, pregnancy, renal/hepatic impairment, and local formularies.

## 5.1 Analgesics & Antipyretics
- **Paracetamol (Acetaminophen):** Adult single dose 500–1000 mg every 4–6 hours as needed; max commonly 3–4 g/day (check local guidance and hepatic risk).
- **Ibuprofen (NSAID):** 200–400 mg every 4–6 hours; typical max 1200 mg/day OTC; higher prescriptions depend on indication—consider GI/renal/cardiac risks.

## 5.2 Antibiotics (selected — examples)
- **Amoxicillin (oral):** Common adult dose 500 mg three times daily or 875 mg twice daily for many infections; adjust for renal impairment.
- **Amoxicillin-clavulanate:** Dosing varies; watch for diarrhea and allergy.
- **Ciprofloxacin:** Use only when indicated; associated with tendon risk and other serious adverse effects.

## 5.3 Antihypertensives (examples)
- **ACE inhibitor (e.g., lisinopril):** Start low and titrate. Monitor renal function and K+.
- **Calcium-channel blocker (amlodipine):** Useful in older patients and certain ethnic groups.

## 5.4 Antidiabetics
- **Metformin:** Start 500 mg once or twice daily, titrate to 2000 mg/day as tolerated; avoid in significant renal impairment.
- **Insulin:** Multiple formulations — dosing individualized per glucose readings and clinical setting.

## 5.5 Antiplatelets & Anticoagulants (overview)
- **Aspirin:** Low-dose for secondary prevention when indicated; balance bleeding risk.
- **Direct oral anticoagulants (DOACs):** Use per indication (e.g., AF, VTE) and renal dosing; specialist initiation recommended.

# 6. Vaccination Notes (Overview)

- Follow local/national immunization schedules. Common adult vaccines include influenza (annual), COVID-19 per latest recommendations, Tdap booster as indicated, pneumococcal vaccines for certain age/risk groups, and hepatitis B for at-risk individuals.
- Pediatric schedules vary by country—follow national EPI schedule.

# 7. First Aid & Emergency Management

## 7.1 Basic life support (adult) — brief steps
- **Check responsiveness.** If unresponsive, call emergency services.
- **Open airway and check breathing.** If not breathing normally, begin CPR: chest compressions at 100–120/min, depth ~5–6 cm, ratio 30:2 for single rescuer; use AED if available.
- **Give rescue breaths** if trained or perform compression-only CPR until help arrives.

## 7.2 Severe bleeding
- Apply direct pressure, elevate limb if possible, use tourniquet as last resort if life-threatening hemorrhage.

## 7.3 Anaphylaxis
- **Recognition:** Airway compromise, facial swelling, urticaria, hypotension.
- **Immediate management:** Intramuscular epinephrine (0.3–0.5 mg of 1:1000 [0.3–0.5 mL] in adults), call emergency services, airway support, oxygen, IV fluids, adjunctive antihistamines and steroids after epinephrine.

## 7.4 Burn first aid
- Cool with running water for at least 20 minutes for thermal burns (do not use ice). Cover with sterile dressing. Seek medical care for deep/full-thickness or large-area burns.

# 8. Laboratory & Imaging Interpretation — High-level Guidance

- **CBC:** Look for anemia (low Hb), leukocytosis (infection/inflammation), thrombocytopenia (bleeding risk).
- **Basic metabolic panel:** Electrolytes (Na/K/Cl), creatinine, urea — assess renal function and electrolyte disturbances.
- **Liver function tests:** ALT/AST, bilirubin, alkaline phosphatase.
- **CRP/ESR:** Markers of inflammation; nonspecific.
- **Imaging:** Use ultrasound for many abdominal, pelvic, and soft tissue evaluations; X-ray for chest and bones; CT/MRI for detailed anatomic assessment when indicated.

---

# 9. Telehealth, Consent & Privacy

- Get explicit informed consent for telehealth encounters; document the consent and limitations.
- Explain that remote assessments have limits and may miss certain findings that require in-person examination.
- Ensure patient data security, encrypted storage, and compliance with local data protection laws.

---

# 10. Red Flags — When to Escalate

- Any signs of unstable airway, breathing, circulation (ABCs).
- Chest pain consistent with myocardial ischemia, stroke signs, severe uncontrolled bleeding, sepsis features (fever + hypotension + organ dysfunction), severe mental health crisis or suicidal ideation.
- Sudden, severe abdominal pain with peritoneal signs, or severe trauma.

---

# 11. Preventive Care & Lifestyle Counseling

- Counsel on nutrition (balanced diet, limit processed foods), physical activity (≥150 minutes moderate-intensity/week for adults), smoking cessation, alcohol moderation.
- Screen for common conditions per age/risk (e.g., hypertension, diabetes, cervical cancer screening where available, colorectal screening at guideline ages).

---

# 12. Safe Use & Limitations of this File

- This file is designed for RAG ingestion and retrieval. It is **not** an exhaustive clinical reference and must be used with caution for any high-stakes clinical decision-making.
- **Do not** allow the chatbot to give definitive medical advice without including disclaimers and encouraging users to seek in-person evaluation for serious or urgent symptoms.
- Implement guardrails: when retrieved content includes treatment or dosing, verify with up-to-date local guidelines before action.

## Appendix A — Example disease entries (expanded)

### Acute Otitis Media (AOM)
- **Overview:** Middle ear infection, common in children.
- **Symptoms:** Ear pain, fever, irritability, otorrhea if perforation.
- **Diagnosis:** Clinical; otoscopy showing middle ear effusion and inflammation.
- **Management:** Pain control (paracetamol/ibuprofen). Antibiotics (amoxicillin) in selected children or when severe; many cases can be observed with close follow-up depending on age and severity.

### Community-acquired Pneumonia (CAP)
- **Overview:** Acute infection of the lung parenchyma acquired outside hospital.
- **Common pathogens:** Streptococcus pneumoniae, atypicals (Mycoplasma), viruses.
- **Symptoms:** Fever, productive cough, pleuritic chest pain, dyspnea.
- **Diagnosis:** Clinical + chest x-ray; consider blood cultures if severe.
- **Management:** Empiric antibiotics guided by local resistance patterns; oxygen for hypoxemia, fluids, and supportive care.
